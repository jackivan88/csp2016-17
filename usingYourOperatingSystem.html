Using Your Operating System

Your computer is running either macOS or Windows. In this lesson you're going to learn some of the basic features of your operating system so that you can work more quickly and effectively.

First, you need to know what operating system you're running, and which version of it. Almost all computers running macOS are manufactured by Apple, and all Apple computers have the signature Apple logo on them somewhere. However, Apple computers are capable of running other operating systems besides macOS. Always remember that hardware is not the same as software. Hardware is the machinery that the software runs on.

If you look at the upper edge of your screen and see a menu bar with an apple at the far left edge, you're running macOS. If you look at the lower edge of the screen and see a square or circle divided into four parts, you're running Windows. However, there are many different versions of macOS and Windows, so move on to the appropriate section and follow the checkpoints to learn more about what your computer is running.

macOS

Checkpoint 1: Which Version?

Click on the apple icon at the upper-left corner of your screen. Choose About This Mac and look for a version number underneath the name. That name and number is the version of macOS (formerly OS X) that you are running.

Checkpoint 2: Lock and Unlock

By default, Apple computers don't have a quick way to lock the screen when you move away from your computer. However, you should know how to enable this so that your computer is always secure when you're not using it. Before you do anything, try pressing Control-Shift-Eject (if you have a CD drive) or Control-Shift-Power (if you do not have a CD drive) and see what happens.

Then, go to the Apple menu and choose System Preferences. Choose Security & Privacy and look under the General tab. You should see a check box labeled Require password... Choose an interval and then close your System Preferences. If you did this correctly, then pressing Control-Shift-Eject or Control-Shift-Power should cause your screen to go black. When you move the cursor or hit a key, it should require you to enter your password to use your computer.

Checkpoint 3: Sleep and Wake

Since you are using a laptop, there are multiple ways to make your Apple computer sleep. Sleep is just a term we use to describe computers that are in a low-power mode that allows them to be used quickly. When you press the power button on your phone to turn off the screen, it is in sleep mode.

Go to the Apple menu and find the option labeled Sleep. Press it and see what happens. Then move the cursor or press a key to wake it back up. Then close the laptop, leave it for about five seconds, and open it back up. Which method would work on either a desktop or a laptop?

Checkpoint 4: Shutdown and Startup

To completely power down your laptop, go to the Apple menu and choose Shut Down. Your laptop should be completely powered off within a few seconds. Then how do you turn it back on?

If your computer was completely frozen, meaning that it won't respond to you, you can force it to shut down by holding down the power button for several seconds. However, this is never a good idea if your computer isn't frozen. Always shut down the computer by choosing Shut Down from the Apple menu.

Checkpoint 6: What's Going On?

If your computer is running slowly or you are concerned there is a problem, you should check Activity Monitor. You can use this tool to see what is running on your computer as well as force programs that are frozen to quit. Click on the magnifying glass in the upper-right corner of the screen and see if you can find the Activity Monitor.

Checkpoint 8: Common Shortcuts

Your Apple computer has several modifier keys. These keys don't do anything on their own (you can try pressing one to see this) but when they are pressed along with other keys they allow you to perform different tasks.

  • Command ⌘
  • Shift ⇧
  • Option ⌥
  • Control ⌃
  • Caps Lock ⇪
  • Fn

You should know these modifier keys and be able to press the right one when asked. Take a look at the first section of Apple's list of Mac keyboard shortcuts (titled Cut, copy, paste, and other common shortcuts) and try each one of them. Once you're done you will be asked to try several at random. Once you can do at least five from memory you'll be able to move on.

Windows

Checkpoint 1: Which Version?

First, press Windows-X on your keyboard. A menu should appear in the lower-left corner of your screen. Choose System. This will bring up your computer's information. See if you can find the name of your operating system. If nothing happened when you pressed Windows-X, then click the Windows icon at the lower-left corner of your screen. On the right-hand side of the Start Menu you'll see Computer as one of the choices. Right-click on it and select Properties. You should be able to see the name of your operating system.
